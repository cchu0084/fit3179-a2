Slip, Slop, Slap, Still? Tracking Skin Cancer in Australia

Mapping how the sun, our habits, and time shape melanoma in Australia.

Melanoma is a malignant tumour that develops from melanocytes, pigment-producing cells of the skin. It is the most aggressive form of skin cancer, with high potential to spread. Melanoma often shows no early symptoms, but warning signs include changes in a mole's colour, size, or shape, or a new spot. Affected moles may become raised, itchy, or bleed.

In Australia, melanoma is a major public health concern, driven by intense year-round ultraviolet (UV) radiation, a largely fair-skinned population, and sun-seeking lifestyles. These factors contribute to melanoma rates that exceed those in North America and Europe, making prevention and early detection vital health priorities for all Australian communities.

Australia's global standing

Data from the World Cancer Research Fund (2022) shows that 331,722 new melanoma cases were recorded globally. Among these, Australia reported 16,819 new cases, representing one of the highest burdens worldwide.

When adjusted for population and age distribution, Australia recorded the world's highest age-standardised incidence rate (ASR), underscoring its exceptional vulnerability to melanoma compared with other nations.

These findings emphasise the ongoing importance of sun protection, early detection, and behavioural change campaigns in reducing future incidence rates.

*ASR is the rate a population would have if it had a standard age structure.

Top cancer incidences & stage distribution

In 2020, melanoma ranked as the third most commonly diagnosed cancer in Australia, with projections indicating it will remain the third most common in 2024.

In 2023, most melanomas in both males and females were diagnosed at an early stage while later stages made up only a small proportion of new cases.

Trend over the years for new cases

Incidence of melanoma in Australia has steadily increased over the decades, with higher rates among males. In 2020, the age-standardised incidence rate was 59 cases per 100,000 persons (73 for males and 47 for females).

The incidence rate for melanoma of the skin is expected to increase with age, highest for those aged 85 to 89 years. This trend highlights the importance of targeted prevention and regular screening among older adults in Australia.

UV Index by state

Australia records some of the highest levels of UV radiation worldwide, largely due to its proximity to the equator and the abundance of clear, sunny days.

The UV Index is a standardised measure used to express UV radiation intensity.

UV exposure is highest across northern Australia and during the summer months, when sunlight reaches more directly and atmospheric filtering is minimal.

At solar noon, values typically fall between 0 and 12, with readings above 11 classified as extreme.

Melanoma develops when DNA damage in melanocytes (the skin cells that produce pigment) goes unrepaired. This damage triggers abnormal cell growth, driven by both genetic predisposition and excessive UV exposure.

Sunscreen behaviour among Australians

It is important to note that regular use of sunscreen is key in reducing the risk of skin cancer.

The best defence starts with UV-protective clothing, complemented by hats, shade, sunglasses, and sunscreen for comprehensive everyday protection.

According to Cancer Council Australia, sunscreen should be applied whenever the UV Index is forecast to reach 3 or higher.

In the ACT, nearly half of residents (49.2%) reported using sunscreen on most days in the past month, notably higher than the national average of 38.1%. In contrast, usage was lower in Queensland (35.3%) and South Australia (35.4%).

Across all states and territories, females were generally more likely than males to use sunscreen regularly.
