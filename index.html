Slip, Slop, Slap, Still? Tracking Skin Cancer in Australia

Age-standardised incidence, mortality and UV exposure visualisation

Melanoma is a malignant tumour originating from melanocytes, the pigment-producing cells of the skin. It is the most aggressive form of skin cancer, distinguished by its high potential to spread (metastasise) to other parts of the body. In Australia, melanoma represents a significant public health burden, driven by a combination of intense year-round ultraviolet (UV) radiation, a predominantly fair-skinned population, and sun-seeking cultural behaviours. These factors contribute to melanoma rates that consistently exceed those observed in North America and Europe, making prevention and early detection key national health priorities.

Australia's global standing

Data from the World Cancer Research Fund (2022) shows that 331,722 new melanoma cases were recorded globally. Among these, Australia reported 16,819 new cases, representing one of the highest burdens worldwide.

When adjusted for population and age distribution, Australia recorded the world's highest age-standardised incidence rate (ASR), underscoring its exceptional vulnerability to melanoma compared with other nations.

*ASR is the rate a population would have if it had a standard age structure.

Top cancer incidences

Melanoma remains one of Australia's most commonly diagnosed invasive cancers; about 1 in 17 Australians will be diagnosed by age 85.

In 2020, melanoma ranked as the third most commonly diagnosed cancer in Australia, with projections indicating it will remain third in 2024.

Despite improvements in early detection and treatment, melanoma continues to pose a significant public health challenge for prevention and screening programs.

Trend over the years for new cases

Incidence of melanoma in Australia has steadily increased over the decades, with higher rates among males. In 2020, the age-standardised incidence rate was 59 cases per 100,000 persons (73 for males and 47 for females). The incidence rate for melanoma of the skin is expected to increase with age, highest for those aged 85 to 89 years.

UV Index by state

Australia records some of the highest levels of UV radiation worldwide, largely due to its proximity to the equator and the abundance of clear, sunny days.

To track UV exposure, the UV Index, a standardised measure of UV intensity, is used. At solar noon, values typically fall between 0 and 12, with readings above 11 classified as extreme.

Melanoma develops when DNA damage in melanocytes (the skin cells that produce pigment) goes unrepaired. This damage triggers abnormal cell growth, driven by both genetic predisposition and excessive UV exposure.

Sunscreen behaviour among Australians

Regular use of sunscreen is a key strategy in reducing the risk of skin cancer, particularly when combined with other sun protection measures. According to Cancer Council Australia, sunscreen should be applied whenever the UV Index is forecast to reach 3 or higher.

In the ACT, nearly half of residents (49.2%) reported using sunscreen on most days in the past month, notably higher than the national average of 38.1%. In contrast, usage was lower in Queensland (35.3%) and South Australia (35.4%). Across all states and territories, females were generally more likely than males to use sunscreen regularly.
